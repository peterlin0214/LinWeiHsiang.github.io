  • Finance
    • Budgeting

      •      Creating annual budgets without previous finance data meant I had to consider annual business plans and related financial activities in close collaboration with the development department and other parties. For example, business development department might have planned to join five exhibitions. By establishing a clear roadmap of what and how we were going to do, I could estimate the revenue and expense and come up with a reasonable annual budget.

      1. Zero-Based Budgeting

             It is obvious that traditional budgeting might not be the best way to create our annual budget, since there was no historical data. Even there was, the financial activities might shift from year to year as the business environment was full of uncertainty. Thus, I used zero-based budgeting as the baseline of annual budget. This was a little time-consuming. However, the budget could better reflect the priority corresponding to our annual business plan.

      2. Activity-Based Budgeting

            Activity-based budgeting was applied after we got some basic data, indicating the allocator(cost driver) regarding their corresponding business activities, or somethings we called "direct cost" . For example, labor-hour was the cost driver to salary and wages expenses. However, this was just a roughly explanation. To be more precise, the activity-based budgeting was mainly used to set the budget with "revenue-generating" activities.

             Thus, classifying business activities into revenue-generating and operating-related categories was the step taken before budgeting. Then, we could calculate the cost per unit by dividing the total cost to the total units of the allocator. After estimating the units required to the future business activities based on our business plans, I could set our budget more precisely. This also helped with the financial analysis in the later part.

             As for the budget of operations side, it included two parts, the indirect costs, which actually have something to do with the revenue-generating activities, and normal operating costs, which were mainly fixed. The indirect costs were estimated to be ratio of direct costs. The indirect costs were relatively hard to be accurately predicted and should be carefully considered.

            Finally, I could come up with a budgeted income statement.

      3. Budget Tolerance Limit

            Creating a solid budget was important. However, with the ever-changing environment, I needed to set budget tolerance limit, primary based on the maximum risk we could take and some data regarding the cost driver we collected, such as the mean value and the deviation of travel expense per trip, to set the tolerance limit for the business trips, for example.

            There were two factors that might affect the precision of the budget, the cost per unit and the units it took as we predicted the business activities. Also, some business activities might cause indirect cost, which was hard to be accurately estimated.

      4. Budget Adjustment

            Another part of budgeting is making adjustment after a period. But first, I had to evaluate and truly understand the performance to make sure the calculation wouldn't be conducted with bias. For example, the utilities expense might be based on a ratio to the total labor hours. A real expense less than expected might not be caused due to a overestimated ratio. Though the total labor hours might be just the same as we predicted, our team could have a ten-day business trip, leaving our office underutilized.

            After examining the meaning and possible reason behind the mis-predicted budget, I could use the data to recalculate the unit cost or the units required to the related activities and adjust the future budget.

    • Accounting

      •      The technical know-how, funding, and the operations team may be the most crucial elements for a startup. However, financial management could play as a equally important role. And a well performed financial management could not be conducted without a precision accounting system.


      1. Managerial Accounting

             Accounting sounds to be the easiest part for the small business, and that's why it is often neglected. However, this is a critical part. Without comprehensively documented transaction records, it will be hard to conduct other financial management tasks, such as financial analysis and budgeting.

             My job was to stick to managerial accounting to make sure all financial activities were properly recorded. Thus, these data could be prepared for further analysis and decision making. Some of the challenge included how to classify direct and indirect cost. Sometimes, the same activities could serve for different purposes, making the boundary vague.

    • Financial Analysis

      •      The financial analysis was the key component of measuring our business performance. The analysis was conducted mainly through the following methods.


      1. Vertical Analysis

             Through analyzing and comparing the real financial situation to the expected one through income statement, I could evaluate the business performance, identify potential risks, and have a better understanding of the cost structure to refine the under-estimated or over-estimated situations. It was important not just looking at the differences between estimation and real-performance but also finding out the reasons behind. Also, it should be conducted with horizontal analysis before making conclusion. I would show an example at the following "horizontal analysis" section.

      2. Horizontal Analysis

             The horizontal analysis provided the basic and intuitive insights of the trend, as well as the opportunities and risks. It was crucial to take the previous periods into consideration while evaluate the business performance. For example, the material, supplies, and wages expense regarding R&D department could be higher during and after the months when we attended exhibitions. The extra expense was resulted from the cases under evaluation and development, which not yet be done and counted as "unearned revenue".

             The vertical and horizontal analysis also interacted with the budgeting adjustment. For example, the utilities expense was correlated with labor hours with certain ratio, which meant using labor hour as allocator was not a problem. However, if the utilities expenses were all much higher than budgets under different labor hours, we must recalculate the indirect cost rate to adjust our budgeting. On the other hand, if there were expenses less than budgets without affecting operating performance, it might indicate there were idle capacities.

      3. Project Analysis

             As our products were software solutions, it was relatively simple for analysis compared to retailers or companies managing inventories. On the other hand, the development cost was the fundamental and critical part as analyzing the business performance. Since every case required different duration and resources, one of my job was to carefully analyze the performance of each project. This went back to the issue to properly allocated the indirect cost hidden behind the business activities.

      4. Other Critical Index

             Analyses about liquidity and other critical index were conducted as well. However, it was not the major part of the financial management tasks.

      5. Alternative Plans

            Budgeting and financial analysis were just basic guideline for the upcoming operation. However, what if the performance was just not good enough to generate necessary cash flow? Should we increase more spending on advertisement and other exposure events, or cut the spending down? When we planned our annual budget, we had a process kind like decision tree and sensitive analysis. Thus, When the performance was severely detached from our expectation, we would consider changing our original plan.

            Still, all decisions should be made with sufficient analysis. Most of the time we would be more cautious with spending. Beyond, it was necessary to have well-informed and comprehensive discussions between inner and outer stakeholders. Most importantly, keeping the team members, including myself, from bias decisions due to polarized team discussion and insufficient and reckless encapsulation.

  • Revenue model and backstage design

      Challenges

      • - No Experience in "Mahjong"
            I had to help design backstage of mahjong game while I hadn't played it before.
      • - Uncertain Revenue Model
            I needed to figure out how to attract and gain dealers with easy-understanding model.
    • Revenue model design

      •     Our business model was using "Player Room" mechanism to achieve viral marketing among mahjong players and sell "Room Card" to create revenue. Main competitors, such as "神來也" and “明星三缺一”, were featuring quick match game. However, there were players seeking more "realistic" playing experience and preferring to play with friends instead of computer players.

          With some market surveysshowing those highly addicted players were around the age of 40~50 and had strong connections among their friends, who also played mahjong, I targeted those players and tried to design a revenue model that would fit our business model.

          There were two primary designs, multilevel direct selling and pyramid structure.

      1. Multilevel direct selling
            This mechanism is familiar to many people in Taiwan. The bonus sound attractive. However, there were problems along with its benefits.
        • Agents might stockpile goods(game cards) and cause unstable cash flow.
        • Direct selling had had bad reputation and been associated with fraud.
        • Backstage would be more complicated due to different bonus calculation within the multiple-level structure.
      2. Dealer pyramid

            This structure was better fitted to our business model. We generally divided our structure into three layers. The bottom were agents, who bought room cards from dealers, or the company if there were no corresponding dealers. The middle layer were dealers, who took greater discount from the company and sold game cards to agents.

            To make this easier to understand, we simply used "gold dealer" and "silver dealer" to represent agent and dealer mentioned above.By applying this structure, there were some benefits.

        • Dealers of each stage could focus on their roles.

          •      Silver dealers had more margin profits; however, they had to deal with customer issues and acted like CSRs. Because players(end-users) were their income sources, they would focus on how to attract more players and stimulate more consumption.

               On the other hand, gold dealers' profits came from play cards sold to silver dealers. Thus, they would focus on how to promote more players to become silver dealers. Because gold dealers got to be silver dealers at the first place, they knew what kind of obstacles silver dealers might encounter and acted like mentors.

        • Dealers can chose their positions

          •      Dealers had to meet certain requirements to go to upper stage. However, we concerned that some dealers might prefer and suit particular stage. Thus, we allowed dealers that is qualified to stay at their original stage. This mechanism helped every dealer to perform better. For example, one could be qualified to move from silver dealer to golden, which spent much less time and effort to deal with players. However, if the dealer was passionate about developing new players, he could chose not to become golden dealer. However, each dealer could only have one identity at one time.

        • Backstage would be more simple.

          •      We didn't have much time to modify the backstage structure. By adopting pyramid structure, our backstage would be simple for dealer. They could view how many cards are in stock, how many cards have been sold to whom within specific duration. Dealers just needed to buy cards from upper dealer and control their stocks. Their profits could be easily calculated. This was a benefit for not only dealers but also the company.

        • Avoid stocking

          •      In traditional direct selling structure, since the bonus is given once the product is sold by subordinates, some would even force their subordinates to stock products, or even create subordinates account that are actually themselves. By contrast, because dealers' profit was gain by margin between cost and revenue, there would be no way to create additional profit by stocking products. This also helped us to have a better control of selling activities.

    • Backstage Design

      •     After surveying our target audience, we realized must of them were at the age between 40~45. Taking the user experience into consideration, I designed the backstage buttons to provide necessary functions as less confusing as possible. The original backstage had 27 buttons for dealers. I managed to reduce them to 18. It was not just downsizing the framework but also being aware of what users, including administrator and dealers, might need. It took many steps and refines to come up with the final solution.

      1. What business goals do stakeholders concern?

        2.      Just like data science, in which the first step is to understand the problems aimed to solve, the first step of designing what buttons were needed was to list crucial business goals(or problems) with priority.

      2. What methods should be used to help gain insights and decision makings?

        3.      After listing the business goals being concerned, the next step was figuring out what kinds of methods or measurements could help stakeholders with decision making to achieve certain business goals.

      3. What data are required

        4.      The last step before designing buttons was to think over the data required to facilitate the measurements.

          For example, the golden dealers might concern the follow problems

        • Find out the main customers(silver dealer)
          > Accumulated purchase amount of silver dealers within specific duration
          > List sorted by number of cards purchased by silver dealers with duration selection
          > Button【 Selling Detail 】
          • Selector(Filter): time duration
          • Output: silver dealer's ID, number of cards purchased, consumption percentage, number of cards consumed
          • Order: number of cards purchased, consumption percentage
        • Solve the stocking issue for silver dealers if there is one.
          > The consumption amount compared to purchase amount
          > List sorted by consumption percentage within specific duration.
          > Button【 Selling Detail 】

          •     As the【 Selling Detail 】button above, it came through a series of process. At first, there were two buttons related to each purpose; however, we found out the table could be combined. There were other buttons for checking the general selling situation or with specified silver dealer.

For example, the instructor might not want to add or change content while some extra information or more detailed explanation might be significantly helpful. My supervisor might have a three-day deadline while the instructor wanted to revise the content, which took more time than that. Communication was really important because there was always trade-off between budget, time and interests among stakeholders.

      3. Constantly Review & Test

        4.      It took weeks or even months to build a new course. It would be too late to find out the problems until it was almost done. I had to communicate with instructors before we made videos to share vision about what we expected. Also, I let my friends or colleagues try some of the features during development process to have some responses. It was important to check if we did it in the right way during the process. Constantly review was definitely necessary.
